Can't Stand the Heat? Stay Out of the Kitchen!

Fire Causes (to Tableau)

Argument

So far, the data has indicated a decrease in total fires but an intense clustering of those fires in the western states. In accordance with the US Fire Administrations existing visualizations, it appears that the highest risk individuals are Californians. In order to understand the types of risks Californians and residents of other western states face, we need to take a deeper look at the particularities of fires. We have to understand that each fire is unique. Each fire has its own set of circumstances and variables. One of the most important factors is the cause.

As indicated by the visualization, Cooking is the leading cause of fires by far. Cooking caused nearly three million fires between 2003 and 2017. That is over two million more incidents than the next leading cause, electrical malfunction. Notably, there were fewer than two hundred thousand fires resulting from natural causes in the period. This visualization clearly illustrates a facet of fires – they are caused by human activity.

The vast majority of fires are the byproduct of human activities which run astray. Cooking, appliance, electrical malfunction, equipment malfunction, and heating are all major causes of fire which result from necessary human activites. Cooking and heating are two of our most important tasks as humans. Electrical and equipment malfunctions are a regrettable side-effect of civilization. This leads to the conclusion that the fire which causes so much anxiety and apocalyptic thinking is closely associated with and perhaps a byproduct of some of the most important human activities.

Details

These figures were created by integrating the residential and non-residential fire causes of the US Fire Administration dataset. The annual sums for each cause category were added together to indicate the frequency of fire by cause over the 2003-2017 period.

Question

The fear of natural fires has been diminished as the data indicates they constitute only a tiny portion of fires. If wildfires are almost exclusively a result of human activity, how can we address the causes to minimize destruction?
