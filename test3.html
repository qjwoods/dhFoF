About

 

I initially selected a dataset and learn to analyze, visualize, and ask questions regarding my relationship to wildfires. Ultimately, the affordances of the dataset led to more questions which it could not answer. I identified a secondary dataset to use in conjunction with the primary dataset. The sets were each produced by government institutions and exhibit high levels of quality and trustworthiness. For the purposes of illustration I identified, consulted, and utilized a less formal list of fires in the United States.

Sources

The initial datset - US Fire Administration Residential and nonresidential building fire and fire loss estimate by property use and cause (2003-2017)

The supplementary dataset - National Interagency Fire Center Year-to-Date Statistics

The tertiary dataset - Wikipedia list of North American Wildfires

This project makes extensive use of public domain and creative commons licensed images – many of which were produced by government employees in the course of fire work. Wikimedia was instrumental in identifying relevant images and easily identifying the intellectual property status. Additionally, relevant tweets, both contemporary to and in retrospective of the relevant wildfire are included.

Process

The data required some massaging in order to create values which were useful for understanding the narrative. Multiple datasets, referenced in the sources section were cleaned using OpenRefine. I manually scraped data from the National Interagency Fire Center Year-to-Date Statistics to incorporate their public tables into my project. Similarly I manually scraped a selection of the Wikipedia list of North American Wildfires to form the basis for illustrations of the phenomena represented in the USFA and NIFC datasets The USFA dataset is divided into residential and non-residential data. It required manipulation to rejoin the fields to form a more complete image. Toward the creation of the map of major US fires, I manually converted locations to latitude and longitude using the Get Latitude and Longitude tool at LatLong.net.

Presentation

I am presenting the narrative of the data using the Publii Static CMS platform. The open source tool provided an invaluable graphical user interface which enabled me to effectively craft and present the story of the data in the form of a static webpage. The page is hosted on github. The data visualizations and map were created using Tableau Software’s free to use product, Tableau Public. The interactive quality of Tableau visualizations allowed for a depth of presentation not available in static visualizations. The Timeline was created using Northwestern University Knight Lab’s open source Timeline JS tool. Their timeline template and tool were invaluable in presenting images and a chronology of recent fires.
